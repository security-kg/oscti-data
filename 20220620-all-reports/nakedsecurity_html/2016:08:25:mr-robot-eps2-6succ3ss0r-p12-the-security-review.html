A hackerÂ cat-and-mouse game

In tonight’s episode we got to see how fsociety’s Mobley and Trenton met for the first time – in a coffee house, playing a cat-and-mouse game, though the mouse might not have realized he was one.

Trenton used a bit of social engineering on Mobley – he didn’t suspect she might also be a fellow blackhat – to innocuously ask what kind of phone Mobley used, and he was only too happy to divulge. Trenton even got Mobley to visit a specific website, one that she presumably owned andÂ could monitor all incoming traffic.

Target’s operating system: Check.
Target’s IP address: Check.
That’s pretty much all the information she needed.

All while they were chatting, we see Trenton’s laptop running in promiscuous mode, sniffing the local cafe’s Wi-Fi traffic with a listener script.

With Mobley visiting the website she owns from his phone, she’s able to quickly obtain his IP address and filter the traffic accordingly. And given that Mobley already told Trenton that he’s a big Android fan, Trenton relied on her knowledge of active Android vulnerabilities and makes an educated guess that his phone was, at the time, vulnerable to the Stagefright vulnerability.

It seems she was right, as she was pretty close to her end goal, rooting Mobley’s phone,Â when Darlene conveniently interrupted the attack in progress. Lucky for Mobley, his Â phone was safe for the moment. ButÂ hopefullyÂ this experienceÂ helped him realize that he fell forÂ social engineering tactics a little too easily.

“We’re burnt.”

We don’t see it for more than a few seconds, but the messaging app Mobley and Trenton use to quickly relay updates isÂ Wickr, a secure messaging app that takes user privacy quite seriously. The app uses end-to-end encryption and has a number of features for even the most paranoid of users, including self-destructing messages, which we saw both Mobley and Trenton use.

Wickr has a stronger track record than many apps of guarding user privacy and disclosing any kinds ofÂ federal requests for information access. So Wickr is a smart choice, but it’s not 100%Â without risk. (Is there anything that is?)

Still, it’s certainly much more private than plain old text messaging, at least.

The walls had ears

A key plot point in this episode was fsociety eavesdropping on a confidential FBI conference call. (Presumably they were able to grab the conference call dial-in information from their earlier Femtocell/in-office hack.) While listening in to a conference call itself isn’tÂ aÂ high tech hack, itÂ can have major security implications.

After all, most conference callingÂ systems are open and easy to access by necessity, so they canÂ be a weak link. All you usually need to gain access is a phone number and call PIN. And most of the time, there’s no way for the legitimate call attendees to know that someone is listening in on a muted line.

If this sounds a bit too easy or far-fetched,Â sadly it’s not. In fact,Â just a few years ago it actually happenedÂ to the FBI and Scotland Yard.Â Hackers obtained the call dial-in information, listened in on a call between theÂ two agencies, and recorded the entire call.
