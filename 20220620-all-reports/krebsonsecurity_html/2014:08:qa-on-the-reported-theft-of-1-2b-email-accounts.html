    Q&A on the Reported Theft of 1.2B Email Accounts

    Q: Ok, but more than a billion credentials? That seems like a lot.

    A: For those unfamiliar with the operations of large-scale organized crime syndicates, yes, it does. Unfortunately, there are more than a few successful cybercrooks who are quite good at what they do, and do it full-time. These actors ‚Äî mostly spammers and malware purveyors (usually both) ‚Äî focus on acquiring as many email addresses and account credentials as they can.     One micro example of this: Last year, I wrote about a botnet that enslaved thousands of hacked computers which disguised itself as a legitimate add-on for Mozilla Firefox and forced infected PCs to scour Web sites for SQL vulnerabilities.

    Q: What would a crime network even do with a billion credentials?

    A: Spam, spam and‚Ä¶.oh, spam. Junk email is primarily sent in bulk using large botnets ‚Äî collections of hacked PCs. A core component of the malware that powers these crime machines is the theft of passwords that users store on their computers and the interception of credentials submitted by victims in the process of browsing the Web. It is quite common for major spammers to rely on lists of billions of email addresses for distributing their malware and whatever junk products they are getting paid to promote.

    Another major method of spamming (called ‚ÄúWebspam‚Äù) involves the use of stolen email account credentials ‚Äî such as Gmail, Yahoo and Outlook ‚Äî to send spam from victim accounts, particularly to all of the addresses in the contacts list of the compromised accounts.
