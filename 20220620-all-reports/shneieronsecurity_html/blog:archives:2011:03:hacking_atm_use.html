Hacking ATM Users by Gluing Down Keys

Clever hack:

The thieves glue down the “enter,” “cancel” and “clear” buttons on the keypad and wait until the customer goes into the bank for help before withdrawing money from their account.

The robbed customers have already punched in their PINs when they realize the keypad buttons are stuck. The unwitting customers either do not know that they can use the ATM touchscreen to finish their transaction, or become nervous when the keypad isn’t working and react by leaving the ATM unattended….

Moderator â€¢ July 20, 2014 1:56 PM

Long before the challenge question existed, it was obvious that there were two different types of advertising comments on the blog.

Type A comments are typically gibberish, random snippets of text, or generic messages that could make sense on almost any thread (“Neat post. There’s a problem with your site in Internet Explorer…”). Often the same comment text comes up over and over, with thesaurus-based substitutions to make it look different (“Neat post. There is an issue together with your blog in Web Traveller…”). They may have other weirdnesses designed to defeat spam filters, such as using Chinese characters instead of spaces. They come in huge deposits that are all promoting the same thing — if you saw two, there were probably a hundred more caught in the spam filter — but from a wide variety of different IPs. These spammers are extremely persistent and change their URLs constantly. An overwhelming majority are hawking designer clothes, purses, shoes, watches, sunglasses, and sports jerseys.

Type B comments are usually singletons, or else small numbers of multiples from the same IP or range. Often, they’re vacuous on-topic comments that suggest the author read only the post headline, then put approximately one second of thought into a reply. Or else they’re straight-up advertisements for the product being promoted, which someone has clearly spent a lot of time on, though that someone may barely speak English. The long ads will sometimes have an e-mail and/or phone number instead of a URL. Type B commenters usually don’t persist, though occasionally one will come back weeks later using the same URL or contact info — they never seem to change them to evade spam filters. The product being promoted can be anything, but common ones include dodgy investments, forged documents, and various questionable or illegal services. Or they’ll be related to the topic of the thread — ads for locksmiths on threads about locksmithing, and so on.

So it’s not hard to guess that type A comments are left by software, and type B are left by people. The fact that the security question stops type A and not type B only confirms that. It’s possible that some type B comments are coming from sophisticated spam software that can solve the challenge question, but I doubt it.
