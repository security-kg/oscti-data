Jeremy H â€¢ October 30, 2006 1:35 PM

The argument that the code-base must be small is a non-starter. It’s long for a reason. Those lines correlate to features. Some are the GUI, some are tallying votes, some are auditing, some are security, some are communication, some are “click here to change languages”, etc.

In fact, if the code-base is forced to be kept small, but the feature list grows, you will find developers taking design short-cuts to keep the LOCs down and you will get messier spaghetti-code which will be harder to verify [remember all the tricks from back when memory was short — self-modifying code, anyone?].

The argument should be: feature lists are kept small, and programs are modularized and robustly designed.

There’s another point about election systems that I don’t think I’ve seen discussed (it is outside of the scope of the linked article.)

Eligibility to vote: any person who claims eligibility to vote, but for who is not on the roll for some reason, should be able to record a vote, and have the vote later counted or not, based on a review of their eligibility. (Possibly one wouldn’t bother, if the contested votes were insufficient in number to change the result.)

E.g. the contested voter fills in a ballot, which is placed in a sealed envelope with identifying information on it. All the contested votes are collected, eligibility determined (done purely on what is on the outside of the envelope plus external evidence.) Then the rejected votes are burned (never opened) and the accepted votes are all opened without looking at them, mixed in a big pile, then counted.

This isn’t perfect, but it is much better than where many legitimate voters are disenfranchised because they were “accidentally” removed from the electoral roll.

My “Dream” voting system:
1) Voter is handed a blank ballot by an election worker.
2) Voter takes ballot to booth with large LED counter over entryway.
3) In the booth there is a touchscreen which allows voter to easily select candidates.
4) After voting, voter puts ballot into machine slot. Machine stamps the precinct number, machine number, and vote onto the ballot, then feeds it back out to the voter.
5) Voter verifies information, requesting a new ballot if any mistakes are noticed.
6) Voter feeds ballot back into machine, where a mechanism behind a transparent window
(A) Stamps it with a unique-per-machine hidden random number, termed the “Fingerprint” – should be at least 12 digits.
(B) scans the ballot.
(C) puts it at a random place in a stack of 20 ballots
(D) retrieves the topmost ballot from the stack
(E) increments the large LED counter above the booth
(F) stamps the current time and LED counter number on the ballot and
(G) returns this ballot to the voter to take home.

Voter can exit the booth and, if desired, compare the counter # on their ballot to the LED counter over the booth, ensuring that both are 1 higher than when the voter first entered the booth.

When voter gets home, she goes to a website* and enters the precinct number, machine number, and Fingerprint of her take-home ballot. She is presented with the:
1: Digital scan of the ballot
2: Interpretation of the vote
3: Chart of # votes cast / time of day for her machine.
4: Chart of # votes cast / time of day for her precinct.

*There would also be a 800 phone number which allows her to get #2 given the information she entered.

Side note: each machine is originally seeded with 20 blank ballots, having only precinct and machine information, but a large “VOID” over the voting area. Early voters who receive one of these need not verify much more than vote counts. At the end of the day, election officials will be responsible for verifying the remaining 20 ballots per booth.
Election officials can also verify that at the precinct level, the total recorded votes is the same as the votes per booth.

“Why can’t the US use the system we use in [insert favorite country here]?”

In the US, each state determines voter eligibility. There is no national person or group saying who can vote and who can’t, or even how it will be done. Also, elections are complicated by local towns and municipalities merging their elections in with national elections. That’s the prerogative of the state to allow this (or not).

Every state has their own voting rules. For example, some states have what they call an “open primary.” In that primary, a Republican can go in and vote the Democratic ballot during the primary if they choose to do so. Open primaries can be “declared” (you have to tell the clerk which ballot to give you) or “undeclared” (you get both ballots, and destroy the one you don’t use). In contrast, a closed primary allows you to vote ONLY in the contests of your registered party. If there aren’t any Republicans running for sheriff, too bad… a Republican don’t get to determine who the sheriff will be.

In some states, like North Dakota, they don’t even do voter registration. They have poll logs/books, and all of the county clerks know all of the voters. This is one of the reasons that ND doesn’t have to do the HAVA-mandated statewide-voter-registration-database that everyone else had to do.

“Why can’t we use a dedicated system instead of general purpose computers?”

If you think election hardware is expensive now, imagine making a system that is built for the rules of only one state. Or, imagine building a system that is flexible enough to accommodate the rules of all the different states, but runs on some kind of non-GP OS. Think that will be cheaper? I think not.

“Why can’t we just go back to paper?”

For most people, there’s probably a variation of this that makes good sense. However, allowing the visually impaired to vote in privacy and without assistance is the biggest issue. We are at a point where a significant percentage of the blind voters in the US do not read the same version of braille. Therefore, if you’re going to do something on paper, you’re still going to run into issues there. In a number of smaller towns, voter privacy and independence are HUGE issues. You may think it’s not a big deal to be the only one voting against the local school tax referendum, but try living in a small town and see if it’s a big deal or not.

It frustrates me to no end that people without a visual impairment take for granted the privacy and independence of voting. This year’s elections are going to be the first where visually impaired voters can cast ballots the way sighted persons have done all along.

(Cognitive impairments and lack of literacy are also an issue here, but there’s only a couple ðŸ˜‰ of systems that really address those issues.)

“Why can’t we use [insert high-tech solution here]?”

Some of the solutions that work in other countries are potentially viable in the US, but the problem is that we have a very NON-TECHNICAL group of people managing our elections. Unless and until the tech-savvy among us decide to get involved by volunteering to work at the polls on election day, we’ll continue to have people who are distinctly non-technical doing fairly complicated things. Until then, the systems used will have to compensate for lack of technical ability.

In addition to all the other comments, there’s a lack of concern voiced here for mis-marked ballots. One of the advantages of an electronic (computer or not) voting system is the ability to prevent you from marking a ballot in an invalid manner. If it’s a vote-for-three contest and you vote-for-four, what can the paper do to warn you that your ballot can’t be counted? Nothing.

In sharp contrast, electronic voting systems can preserve the integrity of the ballot and the integrity of voter intent. (They may or may not do so, but they have the ability.) The infamous “hanging chad” question ultimately came down to “can we determine voter intent?” In many, many cases, they couldn’t. Some of this was a user-interface issue (confusing instructions and poorly marked paper), some of it was operator error. Regardless, the chads couldn’t speak for themselves.

In short, most of the opinions voiced here do not reflect a knowledge of the inherent complexity of the voting process in the US. Bruce, you would do well to do a bit of education to eliminate the misinformation and misunderstandings that are tossed out every time you put up another election-related article.
